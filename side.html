(1) The cognitive science behind the adventures of Junajpu and Xbalamke§

Recently, I secured funding from Cognitive Science Society jointly with my colleague Alex Socop to conduct an outreach activity! We will be conducting an online workshop in which we introduce four cognitive science topics (i.e., neuroscience, cognitive psychology, comparative psychology, and developmental psychology) to indigenous communities in Guatemala using stories from Popol Wuj§. All our materials (in English and Spanish) will be publicly available in CogSci-Maya.


(2) Converting Gregorian dates to the Mayan Long Count

This is a glyph showing
7 k'at of the Cholq'ij
cycle.

The Mayans have a unique way of keeping track of days, in which they count the number of days that has passed since the start of a 144,000-day cycle (i.e., baktun§). At the time of writing this paragraph, the long count is 13.0.11.8.5 (the Cholq'ij§ date being 8 Kan). There are a number of websites which offer the conversion between a Gregorian date and the Mayan long count; however, most of them do not show how the calculation is done. We thus started this project to provide open-source scripts on the Gregorian-Long Count conversion.


(3) Retelling the stories of Popol Wuj


A rough sketch of the four
messengers of Xibalba §.



The Popol Wuj is an ancient Mayan book which tells the tale of the origin of mankind and the adventures of Junajpu and Xbalamke (aka the hero twins). The Popol Wuj was originally written in K'iche', a Mayan language, and has since been translated into other languages, notably in Spanish and English. While scholars and translators rightly focus on ensuring that the translation from K'iche' to another language is as accurate as possible, we aim to retell these Stories from Grandmama in a fun way.
