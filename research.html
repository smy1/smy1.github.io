The ABC of children

A for Acquisition



How do children learn new words? I am interested in the strategies that children use to resolve referential ambiguity, i.e., how children select referents for novel words. My earlier research focuses on the use of Mutual Exclusivity (ME), i.e., the assumption that each object has only one label and thus novel words should be associated with novel objects. We found that even when children rely on ME during referent selection, they are still willing to map the novel word to a familiar, name-known object1, 2. When syntactic cues such as classifiers are available, children also willingly overrule ME in favour of the syntactic cues3. Other than ME, I also looked at whether and how children make use of word form-meaning systematicity durinng referent selection4.

B for Books



What are the benefits of interactive shared reading? Research has demonstrated that shared reading enhances children's vocabulary size because children are exposed to many new words during reading. Shared reading has also been shown to create rich parent-child interaction, which is beneficial to children's brain and cognitive development. C for Curiosity



How do curiosity and interest shape learning? "Curiosity kills the cat, but satisfaction brings it back". Recent studies have shown that children actively seek out information that they are interested in or are curious about. This seems intuitive enough, but we are still trying to unravel the mechanisms behind curiosity, interest, active learning, and children's learning outcome. In one of my projectsยง, we find that the link between active learning and learning outcome is not that straight-forward and may depend on the child's learning preference.
