The ABC of children

My research examines how children learn language from social interaction and exploration — themes I loosely group as the A (Acquisition), B (Books), and C (Curiosity) of children.

A for Acquisition



How do children learn new words? I study how children resolve referential ambiguity, that is, how they identify the meanings of unfamiliar words. My earlier work focused on Mutual Exclusivity (ME), the assumption that each object has only one label and that novel words should therefore refer to novel objects. I found that children are flexible learners: although they often rely on ME during referent selection, they are nevertheless willing to map novel words onto familiar objects1, 2. I also showed that children readily exploit multiple cues during word learning, including grammatical classifiers3 and word form-meaning systematicity4, i.e., the tendency for similar-looking objects to have similar-sounding labels.

B for Books



How does interactive shared reading shape children's development? Across several studies conducted with my principal investigator, we found that early dialogic reading is positively associated with children's later productive vocabulary and sentence complexity1, as well as frontal brain activation during a card-sorting task2. We extended this work in a hyperscanning study3 and found greater parent-child brain synchronisation during shared reading than reading alone. Together, these studies suggest that children's brain and language development can be shaped through simple social interactions such as shared reading. Given the laborious task in coding for interactivity during shared reading, we also developed an AI-based coding programme4 that automatically identifies dialogic reading strategies used by parents during shared reading with infants.

C for Curiosity



How do children decide what information to explore? Infants and children actively shape their own learning environments through exploration and information sampling. In one study1, we found that toddlers tend to initiate joint attention with their caregivers during free-play interactions, suggesting that toddlers often play an active role in guiding caregiver-child interactions. In another study2, I showed that the benefits of active sampling for learning are nuanced: active sampling enhances children's learning only if children are intrinsically motivated to sample objects actively. My future research will further investigate the mechanisms underlying children’s curiosity, information sampling decisions, and learning outcomes.
