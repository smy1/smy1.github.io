Converting between Gregorian dates and the Mayan Long Count

The Mayans have a different way in keeping track of days, in which they count the number of days that has passed since the start of a 144,000-day cycle (i.e., baktun). When they reach the end of the existing baktun, they move to a new baktun (the world does not end with the end of a baktun). This day-keeping system allows them to identify equinox, the right time in planting corn (their staple food), etc.. At the time of writing this paragraph, the long count is 13.0.11.8.5 (Cholq'ij date is 8 Kan).

There are a number of websites which offers the conversion between a Gregorian date and the Mayan long count, however, most of them do not show how the calculation is done. We thus created this little project, to provide open-source scripts (in Python and in R) for those interested in the Gregorian-Long Count conversion. We also provide fun facts in the conversion program to debunk myths and misunderstandings on the Mayan Long Count.


Retelling the stories of Popol Wuj

The Popol Wuj is an ancient Mayan book which tells the tale of the origin of mankind and the adventures of Hunajpu and Xbalamque (aka the hero twins). Popol Wuj was originally written in K'iche', a Mayan language, but has since been translated into other languages such as Spanish and English. It is often regarded as the Mayan Bible, but Mayan locals view the book more as the history of their ancestors.

While scholars and translators rightly focus on ensuring that the translation from K'iche' to another language is as accurate as possible, there is a need to make the text more accessible to children. That is why we decided to retell the tales of Popol Wuj in a child-friendly manner and to make our story freely available for all to read.
